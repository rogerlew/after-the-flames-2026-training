# WEPPcloud and PATH: A Cost-Effective Approach to Watershed Recovery

## After the Flames 2026 — Hands-On Workshop

**Monday, April 6, 1:00 PM – 5:00 PM**

---

This session will provide attendees an overview tutorial of how to use the web-based interface built for watershed modeling based on the process-based Water Erosion Prediction Project (WEPP) model (WEPPcloud) and PATH (Pre/post-fire Assessment of Treatments for Hillslopes). These are two complementary tools designed to identify cost-effective combinations of landscape treatment(s) by hillslope(s) based on user-defined monetary constraints and biophysical targets. The session will be divided between an overview of the tools by the two presenters and subsequent hands-on group work where attendees would have the opportunity to use the two tools with guidance from the presenters.

Post-wildfire landscapes pose serious risks to watershed functions, particularly drinking water quality and soil erosion control. To mitigate these risks, land managers implement hillslope treatments—such as mulching, seeding, or erosion barriers—to reduce runoff and sediment delivery. Hydrologic modeling plays a key role in helping managers identify where these treatments will be most effective following a wildfire. However, these treatments can be expensive, and managers must carefully prioritize where to allocate limited resources to achieve the greatest reduction in post-fire risks.

WEPPcloud is currently used by Burned Area Emergency Response (BAER) teams and other land managers in U.S. to simulate pre- and post-fire hydrology, erosion, and sediment delivery across large landscapes. WEPPcloud automates the acquisition and processing of GIS data for WEPP, automates running the WEPP model, and provides statistical analyses and reports through a web interface. A recent addition to the interface, 'Omni', enables users to run multiple treatment scenarios simultaneously within a WEPPcloud project, greatly improving the speed and efficiency of post-fire assessments. Together, WEPPcloud and OMNI provide spatially explicit estimates of runoff and sediment yields under varying burn severities and treatment options, making them powerful tools for evaluating post-fire watershed responses.

Building on these biophysical outputs, PATH applies a mathematical optimization technique called binary integer programming to identify the most effective combination of hillslope(s) and treatment(s) needed to meet user-specified sediment and water-related thresholds. PATH incorporates user-defined treatment costs and constraints (e.g., slope, burn severity) and supports the evaluation of multiple biophysical thresholds. When a feasible solution exists, PATH determines the minimum-cost set of hillslope–treatment pairings required to achieve it.

The current version of PATH prioritizes mulch treatments (e.g., straw mulch at different tonnage per acre) across western U.S. watersheds, but its flexible structure supports future inclusion of additional post-fire treatments such as seeding, as well as pre-fire options like thinning or prescribed fire. PATH identifies optimal treatment locations based on cost and expected effectiveness. Future developments will broaden treatment types, integrate additional co-benefits, and incorporate user feedback through the interactive WEPPcloud Interface.

Together, WEPPcloud and PATH offer a new framework for integrating hydrologic modeling with economic optimization to guide cost-effective and science-driven post-wildfire land management decisions.
